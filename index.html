Learning foothold-aware quadrupedal locomotion for Unitree Go2 using LiDAR elevation maps and reinforcement learning.

Abstract

Traversing gaps, stepping stones, and narrow support regions requires a quadruped robot to reason about where it can safely place its feet, rather than only recovering after instability occurs. This project develops a terrain-aware locomotion framework for Unitree Go2 that combines a local LiDAR-based elevation map with proprioception and velocity commands in a reinforcement-learning policy. The policy is trained in simulation through a two-stage curriculum: a multi-terrain foundation stage followed by disturbance-aware fine-tuning with height perturbations and touchdown penalties. We evaluate the resulting method on discontinuous terrain families and demonstrate the complete perception-to-control pipeline in both simulation and real-world deployment.

Simulation Training

Phase 1. Train a foundation policy over mixed terrains: gaps, stepping stones, narrow beams, stairs, and roughness.

Phase 2. Fine-tune under ±5 cm terrain perturbations and a touchdown penalty to strengthen unsafe-foothold avoidance.

Deployment Alignment

Deployment preserves the same local-map representation used in training. LiDAR/body-frame transforms and scan-order alignment are handled explicitly so the policy receives terrain in the correct spatial orientation.

05 · CONCLUSION

Project Summary

This work builds a complete terrain-aware locomotion pipeline that links LiDAR sensing, local elevation mapping, reinforcement-learning policy inference, and low-level quadruped control. The key contribution is not simply making the robot walk over rough terrain, but enabling it to use local terrain geometry to distinguish safe support regions from gaps and other hazardous areas.
